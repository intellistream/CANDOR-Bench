\section{Introduction}
Approximate Nearest Neighbor Search (ANNS) is a core primitive in modern data systems, including Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), 
recommendation, and anomaly detection~\cite{Zhong20255017}. Among existing ANNS index families, graph-based indices such as HNSW~\cite{Jayaram2019, Malkov2020824} are 
widely adopted because they offer strong recall-latency trade-offs at scale. Their search process relies on graph traversal over 
non-contiguous memory regions, where random memory access and cache misses often dominate runtime. To improve query performance, 
prior work generally follows two directions: system-level optimizations that make distance computation and traversal faster through optimized storage layout, SIMD, quantization techniques, etc~\cite{Gou2025, Wang2025, Zhong20255017}, 
and algorithm-level optimizations that reduce the amount of computation through pruning and distance estimation~\cite{Chen20233225, Gao2023, Song2025}.

Despite substantial progress, most existing optimizations are developed for static snapshots. 
In practical streaming systems, vectors are continuously inserted and deleted, causing the index topology and data distribution to evolve over time.
This creates a temporal mismatch: pre-computed search hints, i.e., auxiliary information or structures used to accelerate queries, may be 
effective at time $t$ but become stale at time $t+\Delta$, reducing alignment with the current index and degrading query efficiency.
The effect is especially severe in high-rate ingestion workloads, where updates arrive continuously and drift accumulates quickly.
A representative case is real-time payment fraud detection, where incoming transactions are matched against a behavioral embedding database via ANNS under millisecond-level decision SLAs.
In such systems, significant performance degradation across updates is unacceptable because delayed retrieval directly increases fraud leakage risk and financial loss.

To alleviate update-induced performance decay, dynamic ANNS faces a fundamental efficiency-maintenance dilemma, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:motivation}. If search hints are computed once and left 
unmaintained during updates, they may provide short-term gains in early rounds, but their benefit decays over time, causing query throughput to progressively fall back toward baseline performance (Figure~\ref{fig:stale-hint}). In contrast, if the system frequently 
rebuilds or globally re-optimizes them, the maintenance overhead is prohibitive and can dominate end-to-end runtime, limiting overall throughput gains (Figure~\ref{fig:reopt-cost}). Consequently, the key challenge is not merely how 
to accelerate search, but how to keep acceleration \emph{continuously valid} under ongoing updates.



To address this challenge, we propose StreamSeed, a dynamic acceleration framework for streaming ANNS. The key idea is to turn one-shot offline acceleration 
into an online control loop that continuously reuses, validates, and refreshes historical search outcomes as the workload and index evolve. 
Our design is motivated by the observation that, for the same query batch, the top-$k$ results before and after moderate updates often still overlap substantially and the searched vector distributions exhibit a distinct long-tail nature. 
Based on these insights, StreamSeed maintains a bounded seed store as reusable incremental state and continuously writes back effective seeds from recent queries. 
At query time, it retrieves seeds through exact lookup or bounded semantic probing, performs bounded seed-guided expansion, and applies an acceptance-and-fallback gate 
to prevent unreliable reuse from degrading recall.
To keep the design lightweight and practical, StreamSeed further decouples its control policy from backend-specific index internals, which enables integration with different 
dynamic ANN backends at low engineering cost.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item We identify stale-guidance degradation in streaming ANNS and formulate it as an amortized search-maintenance optimization problem, which explains why static accelerations 
lose effectiveness under continuous updates.
\item We present StreamSeed, a dynamic acceleration framework that incrementally reuses and refreshes seeds under workload drift while remaining decoupled from 
backend-specific index internals.
\item We implement StreamSeed on CANDOR-Bench and evaluate it through extensive experiments and ablations. The results show that StreamSeed improves end-to-end efficiency 
while maintaining competitive recall under high update rates.
\end{itemize}
